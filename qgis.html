Landslide Factor Testing

Spatial Analysis of Natural & Human Factors in Idukki District, Kerala
A project by Sara Saju

Problem Statement

Landslides during monsoons destroy homes, farms, and roads in Idukki due to steep hills, weak clay soils, heavy rainfall, and human activities like road cutting and construction.

Existing hazard maps are too general. It’s unclear how strongly factors like slope, soil type, rainfall intensity, and land cover actually correlate with real landslide locations.

Research Question:
How do natural (slope, soil, rainfall, land cover) and human factors spatially relate to landslide occurrence in Idukki?

Data Sources

  • Elevation (30m DEM) – OpenTopography SRTM
  • Slope – Derived from DEM in QGIS
  • Soil Texture – SoilGrids (Clay dominant in south/east)
  • Rainfall – CHIRPS (up to 3,488 mm in high-risk zones)
  • Land Cover – ESA WorldCover 2021
  • Landslide Points – KSDMA + DANS past events

All rasters were clipped to the Idukki boundary, standardized, slope derived, and overlaid with landslide points for correlation analysis.

Methods (Workflow)

Pre-processing:

  • Clip rasters to Idukki boundary using a mask layer (DEM, soil, rainfall).
  • Standardise resolution and projection (EPSG:4326).
  • Derive slope from DEM.
  • Reclassify land cover.

Analysis in QGIS:

  • Overlay landslide points with factor maps (elevation, slope, soil, rainfall, land cover, construction).
  • Validate against GSI/KSDMA susceptibility map (e.g., percentage of landslides in high-risk zones).

Outputs:

  • Factor distribution maps: Land cover, Soil Type, Rainfall, and Slope.
  • Comparison and validation maps: Multi-Factor Landslide Risk Assessment Map & KSDMA Landslide Risk Validation Map.

Key Factor Distribution Maps

Land Cover Map: Tree-covered areas (dark green) dominate the district, with grassland (light green) and shrubland (brown) in scattered patches; water bodies (blue) are minimal and linear; landslides (yellow dots) cluster centrally, often at transitions between tree-covered and grassland/shrubland.

Soil Type Map: Clay (brown) is prevalent in southern and eastern regions, sand (beige) in northern and western areas, silt (grey) in isolated pockets; landslides predominantly overlap with clay-dominated zones, especially in central clusters.

Rainfall & Slope Map: Rainfall ranges from heavy (dark blue, ~3,488 mm) in western/central areas to light (light green/blue, ~482 mm) in eastern edges; slope risk is high (red/orange, up to 2,000) in hilly terrains; landslides appear on moderate-to-high slopes (orange-red) with mixed rainfall intensities, avoiding flat low-risk areas (green).

Multi-Factor Risk Assessment

Overlay of high-risk slope (red), clay soil (brown), and heavy rainfall (dark blue) zones reveals clear clustering of landslide events in areas where all three overlap.

Validation Against Official Maps

The model’s results align closely with KSDMA’s high-risk zones. Integrating soil and rainfall layers refines these predictions, providing more localized and actionable insights.

Conclusion & Impact

Key Findings:

  • Steep slopes (>30°) + clay soil + heavy rainfall = highest risk zones
  • Landslides cluster at forest-to-grassland transition zones
  • Human factors (deforestation, road cutting) amplify natural vulnerabilities

Impact: Enables local planners to prioritize clay-rich steep zones for early warnings, soil stabilization, and reforestation efforts.

Personal Note: This study was inspired by witnessing the 2018 Kerala floods — understanding the link between rainfall and landslides became a deeply personal exploration.
